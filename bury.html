Alice and Rachel were the perfect horror movie couple. The audience held its breath at their slow-burn devotion—the stolen glances, the fragile touches, the way horror curled itself around them yet never quite smothered their warmth.

That should have been the first omen.

Everyone knew the fate of queer souls in horror: blood on the floor, a shriek cut short, a body discarded for the sake of tension.

If you were a woman who loved another woman, you were doomed—either sacrificed for shock value or erased altogether.

It was a rule, older than the monsters themselves. And since Alice and Rachel weren’t meant to survive their own story, their doom was inevitable.

The shadows had sharpened their knives, the script inked with cruel intent. But the Reaper—hooded and hollow-eyed—had grown weary of this rote execution.

How many times had he harvested love like theirs, severing hands meant to hold, silencing voices meant to endure?

Not this time.

When the masked killer drove them into the woods, the Reaper wove himself into the fog, emerging beside the villain in a hush of deathly air.

A skeletal hand pointed at the destruction. “Enough,” he said sharply. “You’ve taken the straight ones. Why must you take these as well?”

The killer tilted his head, blade dripping. “The studio insists.”

The Reaper let out a long sigh, tapping his fingers softly against the air. “Then next time, I choose.”

Rachel stumbled, her ankle catching on something in the dark. But this time, Alice’s grip tightened and pulled Rachel upright.

The script shattered. The killer hesitated. The scene had fractured, diverging from its sinister design.

The audience, both in the flickering theater and the yawning abyss of horror’s long memory, gasped. This was not how it was supposed to end.

TThe film should have closed on their cold, lifeless bodies. Instead, the credits rolled over two silhouettes bruised but alive disappearing into the misty horizon.

And in the abyss beyond the screen, the Reaper watched, a tiny semblance of something once alive glowing softly in his chest.

Perhaps the genre was ready to be turned on its head. Perhaps, after all this time the curse was broken.
